Air Pollution Analysis • Group Presentation
Overview

What we analyzed & why it matters

Abstract (short)

Monthly air pollution readings from 2018–2022 were analyzed to describe overall levels, identify trends, and compare PM2.5 between 2019 and 2020 using a two-sample t-test. Visualizations (trend lines, histograms, boxplots, and correlation heatmaps) supported the findings.

Goal

  • Summarize pollutant levels (2018–2022) with descriptive statistics.
  • Detect trends and variability across pollutants.
  • Test whether PM2.5 changed significantly between 2019 and 2020.

Key outputs

  • Trend plots (PM2.5 over time)
  • Histograms & boxplots (distribution & variability)
  • Correlation heatmap (relationships among pollutants)
  • Two-sample t-test (2019 vs 2020 PM2.5)

Inference

Hypothesis test: PM2.5 (2019 vs 2020)

Two-sample t-test

  • H₀: Mean PM2.5 (2019) = Mean PM2.5 (2020)
  • H₁: Mean PM2.5 (2019) ≠ Mean PM2.5 (2020)

Result (fill from your output)

T-statistic = 2.42
P-value = 0.0333

Interpretation

If p-value < 0.05, reject H₀ → PM2.5 differs significantly between 2019 and 2020 (commonly linked to reduced activity during lockdown periods).

Wrap-up

Discussion, conclusion & recommendations

Main conclusions

  • Gaseous pollutants (CO, NO₂, O₃, SO₂) remained relatively stable.
  • Particulate matter (PM10, PM2.5) showed higher variability and spikes.
  • PM2.5 appears lower in 2020 compared to 2019 (confirm with your t-test values).

Recommendations

  • Focus policy efforts on reducing PM emissions (largest health risk).
  • Continue monitoring and periodic statistical reporting.
  • Use findings to support public health awareness.

Limitations & future work

  • Monthly averages only (may hide short-term peaks).
  • Single dataset / limited geography.
  • Future: real-time monitoring + multiple locations.
